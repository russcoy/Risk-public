The Definitive Guide to US Food Allergen Labeling

A Deep Research Report on Mandatory Compliance and Consumer Protection

In the United States, allergen labeling is not optional. It's the law.

The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA) and the subsequent FASTER Act mandate that all packaged foods must clearly declare the presence of proteins from the most common food allergens. This report breaks down the essential requirements.

The Two Mandatory Compliance Pathways

FALCPA provides two distinct, legally acceptable methods for declaring major allergens. Manufacturers must choose and consistently apply one of these methods on their product labels.

1. Parenthetical Declaration

The allergen's common name must appear in parentheses immediately following its name within the main ingredient list. This must be done for the first instance of the ingredient.

Example:

Ingredients: Enriched flour (wheat), whey (milk), soy lecithin, natural flavor (egg).

2. "Contains" Statement

A separate statement is placed immediately after or adjacent to the ingredient list. This statement must list every major allergen present in the product.

Example:

Contains Wheat, Milk, Soy, and Egg.

The Legal Foundation

Compliance is enforced by the FDA and is based on the following key legislation and regulations. Non-compliance can lead to significant penalties, including product seizure.
